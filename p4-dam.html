Context

I work for an enterprise software company called Perforce, where I was responsible for designing two products within their ecosystem. For this case study, I want to focus specifically on an asset management platform called P4 DAM.

A major challenge we faced was that our delivery capacity was severely reduced. Due to internal redundancies, we went from a team of six front-end developers down to just two, and I was the sole designer supporting the product. Under our traditional workflow, designing static screens in Figma meant impossibly long lead times for implementation.

However, I was granted access to Claude Code with a generous token allowance. This is the story of how I transformed that access into a real, functional system. By handing off functional code instead of static designs, developers could simply review my work and merge it into the quarterly release.

This new process increased feature output by at least 20%, and we expect that capacity to improve by up to 50% for complex features over time.

Initially, when I was introduced to Claude Code, it felt like a completely new ecosystem, as is common with the recent wave of AI innovation. However, I have always been a highly systematic and organised person.

In my personal life, I am the one who organises meetups, keeps everything in order, and plans holidays down to the last detail. This natural inclination benefits my career, as peers often refer to me as a “design systems wizard” or a technical designer.

While many people adopt these new AI tools out of necessity, I genuinely enjoy building systems from the ground up, having previously led and built large-scale design systems for brands like the GSMA and CityFibre. When I was given access to these tools and a strong budget, I found it extraordinarily interesting to explore and engineer entirely new workflows.

The approach

Fortunately, I was able to install the P4 DAM product locally and start building with it immediately.

While other teams in the company struggled with this due to a lack of technical knowledge or other limitations, I got up and running quickly and began editing live product code. I was able to write the code for my designs and ship it directly to developers for review with a minimal bounce rate.

Developers still spent time reviewing the work, but the review process became much more focused and efficient. By providing a higher-quality handover through functional output rather than static designs, there was significantly less iteration overall, with 50% to 60% fewer clarification questions from developers per feature.

Workflow

Consequently, I can manage the front end entirely, taking full responsibility for it while developers only need to review the final output.

With this increased responsibility, I also implemented an automated process to capture visual snapshots of the features as I build them. These snapshots are sent to users to establish a continuous, rapid feedback loop.

Because I can iterate much faster, release more features, and require less rework, the current process is far superior to what it was six months ago.

Wider impact

These workflows and systems I have built are not common throughout the wider design organisation. Typically, most other teams are still working through issues such as how to QA vibe-coded designs or how to get reliable user feedback on AI-generated UI.

In contrast, I am further ahead in this area because I have already moved beyond experimentation and developed a workflow that I know works well for our team.

I am a strong believer that you should not gatekeep these tools or hide any procedural advantage. If something improves quality, speed, or confidence, you have to share it with the team. Things are incredibly competitive in the AI space right now, and to get ahead as a company, we need everyone on board.

Not everyone moves at the same speed or picks up AI as quickly, so working together is essential. I have already mentored several junior and senior designers on how to improve their workflows and AI systems, presenting multiple times over the last few months in quarterly meetings and more sporadically in smaller sessions. The main goal has been to improve the design organisation’s workflows, raise its collective AI skills, and help more people experience those lightbulb moments when a new tip, workflow, or piece of advice suddenly makes the tool feel more accessible and exciting.

As part of this, I recently integrated a workflow that allows designers to go directly from transcribing PM notes into a functional framework to begin prototyping in Claude. This gives designers a clearer starting point, helps them move from raw product thinking into something structured, and makes AI-assisted prototyping feel more practical and repeatable.

Result

The core impact was that I transformed access to Claude Code into a real, functional product delivery system.

By moving from static design handoff to functional code handoff, I increased feature output by at least 20%, reduced developer clarification by 50% to 60%, created a faster feedback loop with users, and established a repeatable workflow that is now influencing how the wider design organisation approaches AI-assisted delivery.

My success with these initiatives caught the attention of company directors, who subsequently appointed me to an exclusive task force. As one of six designers, alongside principals and seniors, I am now responsible for building out the product roadmaps and architecting massive agentic workflows for our entire 30-person design organisation.
