Between Features and Foundations

P4 DAM · Perforce Software · Enterprise Software (Games & Film)

Summary

P4 DAM could store plenty, but finding the right asset again was the bottleneck. While engineering built the platform for Search, APIs, MCP, and AI, I strengthened the discovery layer people used every day: search, filters, saved searches, breadcrumbs, and asset results.

The work made discovery clearer, easier to return to, and less dependent on memory. Search, filters, navigation, and saved workflows now behave more like one system, and the product is better prepared for smarter discovery later.

Context

P4 DAM is Perforce’s digital asset manager for games, film, and media production teams. It helps studios store, organise, review, and manage large libraries of production assets, including 3D models, images, video, audio, and documents.

The product could store a lot. That was not the hard part. The harder part was helping people find the right thing again when they needed it.

At the time, engineering was focused on backend and platform work for Search, APIs, MCP, AI, and future product capability. That work mattered. But users do not experience backend architecture. They experience the search box, filters, results, folder paths, and the moment where they wonder whether they are looking at the right asset.

That was the part of the product I focused on.

The problem

In P4 DAM, discovery is how people get value from the library. If users cannot find the right asset, the rest of the workflow slows down: review, reuse, collaboration, delivery, and production work.

Research and feedback showed the same friction across workflows. Filters were available, but became hard to track once applied. Search had no memory, so users repeated the same queries. Recurring searches had to be rebuilt from scratch. Users lost context in deeper folder structures. Results were harder to scan when the same asset appeared in more than one place.

None of this sounded dramatic on its own. A hidden filter, a repeated search, a missing breadcrumb, or a duplicate-looking result can seem small in isolation. But production work is repetitive, and repeated friction becomes part of how a product feels.

The roadmap was moving toward more capability. Search needed to improve. AI was on the horizon. Customers had real asks. Engineering had real platform work to do. The risk was that new capability would arrive on top of an experience that still made users work too hard.

Design focus

I focused on the parts of discovery people touched every day: search, filters, saved searches, breadcrumbs, and asset results. These were not headline features. They were the parts people used while trying to get somewhere else.

If users cannot see active filters, they start guessing. If they rebuild the same search every week, the product feels forgetful. If they lose their place in a folder structure, they fall back to the browser. If results show the same asset more than once, they compare instead of deciding.

I wanted P4 DAM to carry more of that load. Not by adding a big new layer, but by making discovery clearer, easier to return to, and less dependent on memory.

This is also where the way I work mattered. Because I can work close to the front end, AI and vibe-coding workflows helped me move faster from design direction into working product ideas while engineering stayed focused on the deeper platform work underneath.

What changed

I redesigned filters so applied filters stayed visible, contextual, and easy to remove. Users could see what was shaping their results instead of digging through hidden controls.

I added recent searches so users could return to previous work without typing the same query again. That same thinking led to saved searches, where users could name, pin, reuse, and manage important searches instead of rebuilding the same search and filter combinations each time.

I added breadcrumbs so users could stay oriented in deeper folder structures instead of relying on the browser back button. I also simplified the asset result view. Search could previously show the same asset multiple times when it appeared in more than one place. The new approach showed the asset once, then let users choose where to open it.

Why it mattered

The work was less about making search look better and more about making discovery easier to trust.

Can I see what is active? Can I get back to what I just searched for? Can I save the thing I use every week? Do I know where I am? Is this the asset I need?

When the product answers those questions clearly, users do not have to hold as much in their heads. They need fewer workarounds. They can spend less time managing the interface and more time moving through the work.

That also matters when a product is moving toward AI. Natural language search, AI tagging, suggested filters, related assets, and generated shortcuts all depend on the experience underneath them. If search already feels unclear, AI does not automatically make it clearer. It can make the system feel harder to reason about.

Before P4 DAM could feel smarter, it needed to feel easier to understand.

Outcome

After this work, users could rely more on P4 DAM’s own search, filtering, navigation, and saved workflows instead of working around the product. Search, filters, saved searches, breadcrumbs, and clearer results started to behave more like one discovery system rather than separate pieces of UI.

Engineering was building what the product could do next. Design was strengthening the experience people had to use today. Both mattered. They solved different parts of the same problem.

What I learned

  • A DAM product is only as useful as its ability to help people find the right asset quickly and confidently. Storage alone was not enough. The product became valuable when users could find the right thing, understand what they were seeing, and move into the next part of their workflow without losing context.
  • Small UX gaps compound when users repeat the same workflow every day. A hidden filter, a missing breadcrumb, a repeated search, or a duplicate-looking result might not seem like much in isolation. But in production work, these moments repeat. Over time, they shape whether the product feels dependable or frustrating.
  • Before P4 DAM could feel smarter, it needed to feel easier to understand. AI features would make discovery more capable, but they still depend on the same foundations: clear search behaviour, visible filters, understandable results, and reusable workflows.
  • Design can create progress in parallel while engineering builds deeper platform capability. Because I could work close to the front end, and because AI and vibe-coding workflows helped me move faster from idea to working product, I could strengthen the experience layer while engineering focused on the platform layer.
  • Engineering was building what the product could do next. Design was making the current experience clear enough for that future capability to matter. Design was not just responding to the roadmap. It was preparing the product for it.
